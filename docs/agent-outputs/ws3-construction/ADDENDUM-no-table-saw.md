## Alternative 1: Big Box Store Rip Service

### How It Works

**Home Depot and Lowe's offer cutting services:**
- First 2 cuts free per sheet
- Additional cuts $0.25-0.50 each
- Can rip full sheets to width
- Can crosscut to rough lengths

### Advantages

✓ **Precision**: Professional panel saws, accurate to 1/16"
✓ **Safety**: No handling full sheets on underpowered saws
✓ **Speed**: Cuts done in minutes while you shop
✓ **Cost**: Free or minimal ($2-5 per sheet)
✓ **Clean**: No sawdust in your workshop

### Limitations

⚠ **Quality varies by operator** - Some stores better than others
⚠ **Tearout possible** - May not use fine-tooth blade
⚠ **Accuracy not guaranteed** - Measure every piece after cutting
⚠ **Limited complexity** - Straight cuts only, no dados
⚠ **Store hours** - Must cut during business hours

### Best Practices for Store Cuts

**1. Bring detailed cut list:**
```
SHEET 1 - Cabinet Sides
- Rip to 14" width (full length)
- Then crosscut into six 34.5" pieces

SHEET 2 - Cabinet Tops/Bottoms
- Rip to 14" width
- Cut three pieces at 29.25" (for 30" cabinet)
- Cut three pieces at 31.25" (for 32" cabinet)
```

**2. Verify measurements BEFORE leaving store:**
- Bring tape measure
- Check every dimension
- Request re-cut if off by more than 1/16"

**3. Request finest blade available:**
- Ask: "Can you use your fine-tooth blade for plywood?"
- Reduces tearout on face veneer
- Not all stores have this, but worth asking

**4. Specify good face orientation:**
- "Please keep the good face up"
- Reduces tearout on visible side
- Mark good face with tape before handing to cutter

**5. Go during slow times:**
- Weekday mornings less rushed
- Operator takes more care
- Less chance of errors

### What to Have Store Cut

**DO have store cut:**
- ✓ Rip cuts to 14" cabinet depth (hardest cut to DIY)
- ✓ Initial breakdown of full sheets to manageable sizes
- ✓ Large living room panel (92" × 34.5")

**DON'T have store cut:**
- ✗ Final precision cuts (do yourself with circular saw)
- ✗ Cuts requiring perfect square (cabinet tops/bottoms final size)
- ✗ Any cut where 1/16" matters

### Cost Analysis

**Scenario: 4 sheets of plywood**
- Sheet 1: 1 rip + 6 crosscuts = $0 + $2.50 = $2.50
- Sheet 2: 1 rip + 6 crosscuts = $0 + $2.50 = $2.50
- Sheet 3: 1 rip + 1 crosscut = $0 + $0 = FREE
- Sheet 4 (1/4" backs): 3 cuts = $1.50

**Total: ~$7 for all store cuts**

Compare to table saw investment: $300-800

## Alternative 2: Circular Saw with Track Guide

### Tools Required

**Essential:**
- 7¼" circular saw (likely already own)
- Track guide system OR DIY straightedge

**Track Guide Options:**

**Budget DIY Guide ($20-40):**
- 8' length of ¾" plywood (4" wide strip)
- 8' straight aluminum angle or factory edge
- Clamps
- Build once, use forever

**Mid-Range: Kreg Rip-Cut ($40-60):**
- Attaches to circular saw
- Adjustable fence for rip cuts
- Adequate for cabinet work
- Limited to ~24" rip width

**Premium: Kreg Accu-Cut ($120) or Festool Guide Rail ($150-200):**
- Professional-grade accuracy
- Anti-splinter strips reduce tearout
- May be overkill for one project

**RECOMMENDATION: DIY straightedge + Kreg Rip-Cut combo ($60 total)**

### DIY Straightedge Guide - Build Instructions

**Materials:**
- One 8' × 4" strip of ¾" plywood (factory edge)
- One 8' × 6" strip of ¼" plywood or hardboard
- Wood glue
- 1" screws

**Build Process:**

1. **Attach narrow strip to wide base:**
   - Place 4" strip on 6" base, aligned on one edge
   - Leave 2" overhang on one side
   - Glue and screw together

2. **Trim to exact offset:**
   - Place circular saw on guide, base against 4" fence
   - Run saw along fence, trim off overhang
   - The cut edge is now EXACT cutting line

3. **Use the guide:**
   - Align trimmed edge with cut mark on plywood
   - Clamp in place
   - Run saw along fence
   - Saw blade cuts exactly where you marked

**Benefits:**
- $20 in materials
- Accurate to 1/32"
- Long enough for most cuts
- Can build multiple for different lengths

### Circular Saw Cutting Technique

**For Clean Cuts:**

1. **Use fine-tooth blade:**
   - 60-tooth plywood blade for circular saw
   - Freud Diablo or DeWalt brands
   - $25-40 investment
   - CRITICAL for clean cuts

2. **Score cut line first:**
   - Use utility knife and straightedge
   - Score 2-3 times along cut line
   - Breaks veneer fibers, prevents tearout
   - Extra 2 minutes, professional results

3. **Good face down:**
   - Circular saws cut upward (blade rotates up into material)
   - Tearout happens on top face
   - Place good face DOWN on sawhorses
   - Opposite of table saw technique

4. **Multiple pass option for thick materials:**
   - Set blade depth to ¼" deep first pass
   - Scores cut, prevents tearout
   - Second pass at full depth cuts through
   - Takes longer but cleaner result

5. **Support both sides of cut:**
   - Use two sawhorses
   - Support both pieces being cut
   - Prevents binding and kickback
   - Prevents offcut from breaking away (causes tearout)

### What Cuts Work Best with Circular Saw

**EXCELLENT for:**
- Crosscuts on long boards (cabinet sides to length)
- Breaking down full sheets to manageable sizes
- Notching for column or obstacles
- Living room panel to final size

**ADEQUATE for:**
- Rip cuts with guide system
- Cabinet tops and bottoms to width

**CHALLENGING:**
- Long rip cuts on full sheets (awkward, need help)
- Repetitive identical cuts (setup time each cut)

### Safety Critical

⚠ **Support workpiece properly** - Both sides of cut
⚠ **Clamp guide securely** - Guide shifts = ruined cut + danger
⚠ **Keep cord out of cut path** - Easy to cut your own cord
⚠ **Let blade reach full speed before cutting** - Prevents kickback
⚠ **Don't force the saw** - Let blade do the work

## Alternative 3: Miter Saw for Crosscuts

### You Already Have Miter Saw

**Excellent for:**
- Crosscutting cabinet sides to 34.5" height
- Crosscutting 2×4 for toe kick
- Any cut 12" wide or less (depends on saw capacity)

**Not suitable for:**
- Rip cuts (wrong orientation)
- Pieces wider than saw capacity
- Living room panel (92" too long)

### Miter Saw Technique for Cabinet Parts

**For pieces wider than saw capacity:**

**Method: Score and Snap**
1. Cut as deep as saw allows
2. Flip piece, align cut
3. Cut from other side
4. Cuts meet in middle
5. Clean up with hand plane or sanding

**Method: Multiple Passes**
1. Make first cut at full depth
2. Slide piece over
3. Make second overlapping cut
4. Continue until through
5. Clean up edge

### Miter Saw + Store Rips = Winning Combo

**Workflow:**
1. Have store rip sheets to 14" width
2. Bring home 14" × 96" strips
3. Crosscut to length on miter saw
4. Perfect cabinet sides: 14" × 34.5"

**Advantages:**
- Miter saw excels at repetitive crosscuts
- Stop block ensures identical pieces
- Safer than circular saw for small pieces
- Cleaner cuts (more blade teeth in contact)

## Recommended Hybrid Approach

### The Best of All Methods

**Step 1: Store rips (Home Depot/Lowe's)**
- Rip all sheets to 14" width (cabinet depth)
- Rough crosscut to manageable lengths (36-40")
- Cost: ~$7 total

**Step 2: Final crosscuts (Your miter saw)**
- Cut 14" strips to final 34.5" length (cabinet sides)
- Cut cabinet tops/bottoms to final width
- Use stop block for repeatability

**Step 3: Living room panel (Circular saw + guide)**
- Final cut to 92" × 34.5"
- Use DIY guide or track system
- This is visible focal point, worth extra care

**Step 4: Notches and special cuts (Circular saw or jigsaw)**
- Column notches
- Toe kick cutouts
- Any non-rectangular cuts

### Why This Works

✓ **Leverages strengths of each method**
✓ **Minimizes weaknesses**
✓ **Cost-effective** (~$100 vs. $500+ table saw)
✓ **Safe** (manageable piece sizes)
✓ **Accurate** (right tool for each cut)

## Cut-by-Cut Method Guide

### Cabinet Sides (6 pieces, 14" × 34.5")

**METHOD 1 (Recommended):**
1. Store rips sheet to 14" width
2. Brings home 14" × 96" strip
3. Miter saw crosscut to 34.5" (6 pieces)
4. Use stop block for identical length

**METHOD 2 (All DIY):**
1. Circular saw + rip guide to 14" width
2. Circular saw + straightedge to 34.5" length
3. More time, same result

**Time comparison:**
- Method 1: 20 minutes (after store cuts)
- Method 2: 60 minutes (all cuts at home)

### Cabinet Tops/Bottoms (6 pieces, 14" × width varies)

**METHOD (Recommended):**
1. Store rips to 14" width
2. Measure actual cabinet box width (after assembly)
3. Miter saw or circular saw to final width
4. Cut ¼" less than opening for easy fit

### Living Room Panel (1 piece, 92" × 34.5")

**METHOD:**
1. Store crosscuts sheet to ~36" × 96"
2. Circular saw + straightedge to final 92" length
3. Circular saw + straightedge to 34.5" width
4. Score cut lines with utility knife (this is visible!)
5. Good face DOWN (circular saw tearout on top)

**Alternative:**
- Ask store to cut to final dimensions
- Verify measurements before leaving
- May need touch-up cuts at home

### Toe Kick Platform (2×4 frame + plywood top)

**METHOD:**
1. Miter saw for all 2×4 cuts (it excels here)
2. Store rips plywood to 14" width
3. Ask store to crosscut to 92" length
4. OR circular saw to 92" at home

### Drawer Boxes (if building, see drawer-construction.md)

**METHOD:**
1. Store rips to drawer depth (12-13")
2. Miter saw crosscuts to drawer height (front/back)
3. Miter saw crosscuts to drawer width (sides)

### Barn Door (30" × 30", see barn-door-guide.md)

**METHOD:**
1. Store crosscuts ¾" plywood to 32" × 32"
2. Circular saw + straightedge to final 30" × 30"
3. Extra material allows perfect final cut

### X-Frame Pieces (if using, see x-frame-panels.md)

**METHOD:**
1. Miter saw for all 1×4 or 1×6 lumber cuts
2. 45° miters for frame corners
3. Circular saw for diagonal X-braces

## Troubleshooting Alternative Methods

### Problem: Store cut is 1/8" off

**Solution:**
- Use circular saw to make final trim cut
- Re-cut to correct dimension at home
- Why hybrid approach is best (store rough, DIY final)

### Problem: Circular saw cut not straight

**Causes:**
- Guide not clamped securely
- Saw wandered off guide
- Blade dull

**Solutions:**
- Clamp guide at both ends AND middle
- Keep saw base flat against guide
- Replace blade if dull
- Practice on scrap first

### Problem: Tearout on plywood edge

**Solutions:**
- Score cut line with utility knife (prevents tearout)
- Use 60+ tooth blade (more teeth = cleaner cut)
- Good face down for circular saw cuts
- Slower feed rate (let blade do the work)
- Tape over cut line with painter's tape

### Problem: Circular saw binds in cut

**Causes:**
- Insufficient support (piece sags, pinches blade)
- Cutting uphill (blade fights you)
- Dull blade

**Solutions:**
- Support both sides of cut with sawhorses
- Cut on level surface
- Use helper to support large pieces
- Replace blade
