# Construction Best Practices

**Synthesized Insights for Kitchen Bar Cabinet Build**

This document captures hard-won wisdom from professional cabinetmakers, research, and cross-referencing all work streams. Follow these practices to avoid common mistakes and achieve professional results.

---

## Part 1: Assembly Order Optimization

### Why Order Matters

Cabinet construction is not linear - certain decisions lock in others. The order below is optimized for:
- Maximum access during each step
- Correction opportunities before permanent commitment
- Efficient use of drying and curing times

### The Optimal Order

```
1. Toe kick platform (can level independently)
       ↓
2. Cabinet bottom + dividers (establishes grid)
       ↓
3. Cabinet top (locks dividers in place)
       ↓
4. Verify square (STOP if not perfect)
       ↓
5. Living room panel (structural backing)
       ↓
6. Drawer boxes (separate from cabinet)
       ↓
7. X-frame panels and doors (separate from cabinet)
       ↓
8. Prime and paint (while separate)
       ↓
9. Install cabinet in position
       ↓
10. Hardware installation (slides, hinges, track)
       ↓
11. Attach drawer fronts (applied front method)
       ↓
12. Electrical integration
       ↓
13. Final adjustments and touch-up
```

### Critical Order Decisions Explained

**Why Toe Kick First:**
By building a separate toe kick platform, you can level it perfectly before the heavy cabinet goes on top. Trying to level a 100+ pound cabinet is frustrating and imprecise. The platform approach is how professional kitchen installers work.

**Why Living Room Panel Before Hardware:**
The barn door track mounts to this panel. It provides the structural backing for the track screws. Install the panel, verify it's solidly attached, then mount the track.

**Why Paint Before Installation:**
You cannot properly paint the interior of an installed cabinet. Pre-finishing interiors before assembly is the only way to get complete coverage. Paint the exterior while the cabinet is accessible on sawhorses.

**Why Hardware Before Drawer Fronts:**
Drawer fronts are applied (screwed from inside) after the slides are installed and working. This allows perfect alignment adjustment. If you attach fronts first, you can't adjust alignment.

## Part 2: Tool Setup Efficiency

### The Tool Change Problem

Every tool change costs 10-15 minutes:
- Put current tool away
- Find next tool
- Set it up
- Make test cuts/holes
- Adjust if needed

For 10 tool changes, that's 2-3 hours lost. The solution: **batch by tool, not by part.**

### Tool Batching Strategy

#### Circular Saw Session (Do All Rip Cuts Together)
1. Set up cutting guide for first width
2. Make ALL cuts at that width
3. Adjust guide for next width
4. Make ALL cuts at that width
5. Continue until all widths complete
6. PUT SAW AWAY

**DO NOT** cut one piece, switch to miter saw, cut another piece, go back to circular saw.

#### Miter Saw Session (Do All Crosscuts Together)
1. Set saw to 90 degrees
2. Cut ALL 90-degree crosscuts (organize by length)
3. Set saw to 45 degrees
4. Cut ALL 45-degree miters
5. PUT SAW AWAY

#### Pocket Hole Session (Do All Drilling Together)
1. Set jig for 3/4" material
2. Drill ALL 3/4" pocket holes in ALL pieces
3. Set jig for 1/2" material
4. Drill ALL 1/2" pocket holes in ALL pieces
5. PUT JIG AWAY

#### Sanding Session (Do All Sanding Together)
1. 120 grit: Sand ALL pieces
2. 150 grit: Sand ALL pieces
3. 220 grit: Sand ALL visible surfaces
4. Vacuum ALL dust
5. PUT SANDER AWAY

### Tool Setup Checklist

Before starting each tool session:

**Circular Saw:**
- [ ] Sharp 40+ tooth blade?
- [ ] Cutting guide clamped securely?
- [ ] Sacrificial board under workpiece?
- [ ] Dust collection/vacuum ready?

**Miter Saw:**
- [ ] Blade square to fence (verify with test cut)?
- [ ] Support stands for long pieces?
- [ ] Stop block for repetitive cuts?

**Pocket Hole Jig:**
- [ ] Drill bit sharp (no ragged holes)?
- [ ] Jig set for correct material thickness?
- [ ] Collar adjusted for plywood (slightly shallower)?
- [ ] Driver ready with pocket screw bit?

## Part 3: Batch Processing Opportunities

### What to Batch

| Operation | Batch Together | Time Saved |
|-----------|---------------|------------|
| Rip cuts (same width) | All cabinet panels at 14" | 30 min |
| Crosscuts (same length) | All dividers at 34.5" | 20 min |
| Pocket holes (same material) | All 3/4" pieces | 45 min |
| Sanding | All pieces before ANY finishing | 30 min |
| Priming | All pieces in one session | 30 min |
| Painting | All pieces in one session | 30 min |

### Sample Batch Workflow: Pocket Holes

**Wrong Way (Piece by Piece):**
1. Pick up cabinet bottom
2. Drill 10 pocket holes
3. Put down cabinet bottom
4. Pick up cabinet top
5. Drill 10 pocket holes
6. Put down cabinet top
... (repeat 15+ times)

**Right Way (Batch):**
1. Stack all 3/4" pieces near jig
2. Drill all holes in cabinet bottom
3. Move to next piece in pile
4. Drill all holes in cabinet top
5. Continue through entire stack
6. Reset jig for 1/2" material
7. Process all 1/2" pieces
8. DONE with pocket holes

**Time Difference:** 2 hours vs. 45 minutes

## Part 4: Quality Checkpoints

### The 96% Confidence Rule

Before marking any step "complete," you must be 96% confident it's right. That means:
- You've measured twice
- You've verified with a second method (e.g., square AND diagonals)
- You can't think of anything that would make it wrong

### Checkpoint 1: After Cutting

**Before leaving the shop:**
- [ ] All pieces labeled (which part, which face)?
- [ ] Critical dimensions within 1/16"?
- [ ] Matching pieces identical (stack and compare edges)?
- [ ] No tear-out on visible edges?

**If something is wrong:** Recut now. You have all the tools set up. It's the cheapest fix.

### Checkpoint 2: After Pocket Holes

**Before assembly:**
- [ ] All holes on correct face (will be hidden)?
- [ ] No blowout on opposite face?
- [ ] Holes at least 2" from ends (prevents splitting)?
- [ ] Holes deep enough (screw head will seat flush)?

**If something is wrong:** Drill new holes nearby. Fill bad holes with wood filler if visible.

### Checkpoint 3: After Box Assembly (THE BIG ONE)

**Before installing living room panel:**
- [ ] Cabinet bottom perfectly flat (use straightedge)?
- [ ] All four corners square (framing square test)?
- [ ] Diagonals equal within 1/4"?
- [ ] All dividers plumb (level test)?
- [ ] Cabinet bottom and top exactly parallel?
- [ ] Total dimensions within 1/8" of spec?

**If something is wrong:** STOP. Disassemble if necessary. A crooked box cannot be fixed later. Every subsequent step will amplify the error.

### Checkpoint 4: Before Finishing

- [ ] All surfaces sanded smooth (no machine marks)?
- [ ] All edges eased (no sharp corners)?
- [ ] All holes filled?
- [ ] All dust removed (vacuum + tack cloth)?
- [ ] No glue residue visible?

**If something is wrong:** Fix it now. Paint won't hide flaws - it highlights them.

### Checkpoint 5: After Hardware Installation

- [ ] All drawer slides level (both sides at same height)?
- [ ] Drawers open and close smoothly?
- [ ] Soft-close engages consistently?
- [ ] All doors align (consistent gaps)?
- [ ] Barn door slides freely full length?

**If something is wrong:** Adjust now. Hardware has adjustment screws for a reason.

## Part 5: Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Plywood-Specific Mistakes

**Mistake: Using wrong pocket screw length**
- Problem: 3/4" plywood is actually ~23/32" thick
- Result: Screw pokes through surface
- Fix: Set drill collar shallower than material thickness, test on scrap

**Mistake: Pocket holes too close to edge**
- Problem: Screws can split thin edge
- Result: Weak joint, visible damage
- Fix: Keep pocket holes at least 2" from panel edge

**Mistake: Pocket holes on wrong face**
- Problem: Holes visible after assembly
- Result: Ugly, unprofessional appearance
- Fix: Mark "inside" face on every piece before drilling

**Mistake: Not supporting plywood during cuts**
- Problem: Plywood falls/breaks at end of cut
- Result: Tear-out, damaged piece
- Fix: Use sacrificial board under cut line, support both sides

### Assembly Mistakes

**Mistake: Not dry-fitting first**
- Problem: Discover fit issues after glue is applied
- Result: Rushed, stressed assembly
- Fix: Always dry fit first, make adjustments, THEN glue

**Mistake: Ignoring out-of-square**
- Problem: "It's close enough"
- Result: Doors don't fit, drawers bind, countertop rocks
- Fix: Achieve square or disassemble and fix

**Mistake: Over-tightening pocket screws**
- Problem: Strips the hole, splits wood
- Result: Weak joint
- Fix: Use drill, not impact driver. Stop when snug.

**Mistake: Rushing glue cure time**
- Problem: Joint stressed before cure
- Result: Joint failure, gaps
- Fix: Wait 30 min before handling, 24 hours before heavy stress

### Finishing Mistakes

**Mistake: Painting over glue squeeze-out**
- Problem: Paint won't adhere to dried glue
- Result: Paint peels in spots
- Fix: Wipe squeeze-out immediately with wet rag, or sand off dry glue before painting

**Mistake: Skipping primer**
- Problem: Paint absorbs unevenly, requires more coats
- Result: Blotchy finish, wasted paint
- Fix: Always prime. Shellac-based primer (Zinsser BIN) seals best.

**Mistake: Painting joints that will be glued**
- Problem: Glue won't bond to painted surfaces
- Result: Weak joint
- Fix: Mask joint areas with painter's tape before painting

**Mistake: Not sanding between coats**
- Problem: Rough finish, dust nibs
- Result: Unprofessional appearance
- Fix: Light sand with 320 grit between coats

## Part 6: Time-Saving Techniques

### Store Cutting Service

**Most impactful time saver for no-table-saw builders:**
- Home Depot / Lowe's will rip plywood to width
- First 2 cuts usually free, then ~$0.50 per cut
- Cost: $5-10 for all rips
- Time saved: 2-3 hours
- Accuracy: Often better than DIY circular saw

**What to request:**
- Bring cut list showing widths needed
- Ask for widths, not lengths (crosscuts are easy with miter saw)
- Accept pieces slightly oversized (you can trim)

### Stop Block for Repetitive Cuts

When cutting multiple pieces to same length:
1. Cut first piece carefully to exact length
2. Clamp stop block to miter saw fence at that position
3. All subsequent pieces: push against block, cut
4. All pieces identical - no measuring after first cut

**Time saved:** 1-2 minutes per cut
**Accuracy:** All pieces exactly same length

### Template for Repetitive Drilling

For pocket holes or hardware holes in same position on multiple pieces:
1. Make template from scrap (or cardboard for hardware)
2. Mark hole positions on template
3. Position template on each workpiece
4. Drill through template marks
5. All pieces identical

**Use for:**
- Pocket hole positions on drawer parts
- Hinge cup positions on doors
- Handle hole positions

### Pre-Drive Screws

For difficult-access screw locations:
1. Pre-drive screw until tip just pokes through
2. Position parts
3. Drive screw home
4. Repeat

**Avoids:** Fumbling with screw while holding parts together

### Assembly Jig for Drawer Boxes

Make a simple jig for consistent drawer assembly:
1. Cut two pieces of scrap to drawer box height (10")
2. Nail together in L-shape
3. Use as a corner support when attaching sides to front/back
4. Holds pieces at 90 degrees while you drive screws

## Part 7: Money-Saving Techniques

### Material Optimization

**Full Sheet Utilization:**
- Plan cuts to use entire sheets
- Nest parts efficiently (see WS4 plywood layout)
- Use offcuts for cleats, supports, test pieces

**Standard Sizes:**
- Design to standard lumber sizes when possible
- 1x4s are cheap - avoid ripping 1x6s when 1x4 works

### Hardware Value Engineering

**Drawer Slides:**
- Budget pick (Liberty @ $12/pair) is adequate for this application
- Premium slides ($35+/pair) not necessary for kitchen bar cabinet
- Soft-close is worth the upgrade (+$5/pair)

**Hinges:**
- European cup hinges (~$5/pair) easier to install and adjust than butt hinges
- Don't buy expensive German hinges for painted cabinets

**Barn Door Hardware:**
- Budget kits ($50-60) work fine for cabinet-weight doors
- Premium hardware ($100+) is for full-size interior doors

### Paint Economy

**One Gallon Strategy:**
- 1 gallon primer + 1 gallon paint = enough for entire project
- Buy quality paint (Sherwin-Williams, Benjamin Moore) - better coverage
- Don't buy separate "cabinet paint" - standard interior semi-gloss works

### Avoid Specialty Tools

**You Don't Need:**
- Table saw (store cuts plywood, circular saw for remaining rips)
- Dado blade (pocket holes or router for dados)
- Brad nailer (can use finish nails + hammer, just slower)
- Pin nailer (brad nailer is more versatile)

## Part 8: "If I Could Do It Again" Insights

These are the revelations that only come from completing a project:

### On Planning

> "Measure the actual space three times before cutting anything. My kitchen was 1/4" different than the plans."

> "Order hardware first. Everything else can wait, but a 5-day shipping delay on slides can cost you a weekend."

### On Cutting

> "The store rip cuts are the best money I spent. My cuts were never as straight or consistent."

> "Label everything. I thought I'd remember which piece was which. I didn't."

### On Assembly

> "Dry fit everything first. The 10 minutes feels wasteful until you find a problem."

> "I spent 30 minutes getting the first corner perfectly square. Worth every second."

### On Finishing

> "Sand the inside faces before assembly. I learned this by trying to sand inside a finished box. Impossible."

> "Primer is not optional. The paint jobs I tried to skip primer on haunt me."

### On Hardware

> "Install one drawer slide pair perfectly. Then use that as the reference for the others."

> "The soft-close mechanism on the barn door was a $10 decision that I appreciate every single day."

### On Time

> "It always takes longer than you think. Plan for 150% of your estimate."

> "Weekend projects need stopping points. Don't start glue-up 30 minutes before you need to leave."

## Part 9: The Mindset of Quality

### Craftsmanship vs. Perfection

This cabinet will be a permanent fixture in your home. It doesn't need to be perfect, but it needs to be well-made. The difference:

**Perfection (Wasteful):**
- Sanding invisible surfaces to 400 grit
- Obsessing over 1/64" variations
- Buying premium hardware for hidden applications

**Craftsmanship (Valuable):**
- Every visible surface smooth and well-finished
- Box perfectly square (affects function)
- Hardware operates smoothly
- Joints tight with no gaps

### The Pride Test

After installation, you should be able to:
- Show it to guests without apologizing for anything
- Open every drawer and door confidently
- Know it will work well for 20+ years
- Feel you did your best work

### When Good Enough Is Good Enough

Inside the cabinet (behind closed doors):
- 1 coat of paint is fine
- Minor imperfections acceptable
- Functional > beautiful

Outside the cabinet (visible):
- 2 coats minimum
- Gaps filled and smooth
- Beautiful AND functional
